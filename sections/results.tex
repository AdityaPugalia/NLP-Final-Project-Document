\section{Results}

\subsection{Activation Functions}
Figure~\ref{fig:act_chatcore} shows the ChatCORE and ChatCORE-Categorical training curves across
all activation function variants and the MTP experiment over 5{,}000 iterations.
The NanoChat baseline with $\mathrm{ReLU}^2$ achieves the highest final ChatCORE score,
outperforming all alternative activations on the aggregate metric.

  \label{fig:act_chatcore}

While the evolutionary-search activations (GeLUSine and GSP) showed gains on computer vision
benchmarks \parencite{vitvitskyi2026mininggeneralizableactivationfunctions}, these benefits do
not transfer to small-scale autoregressive language modelling.

The superior performance of NanoChat's original activation (RELU$^2$) over the other activation functions can be attributed to a variety of reasons. RELU$^2$ was found to induce a higher degree of activation sparsity than GeLU in autoregressive language models, and this sparsity has been linked to more efficient gradient flow and improved loss scaling \parencite{so2021primer}.GeLUSine and GSP were optimised for vision, where inputs have smooth spatial frequency structure. Language tokens are discrete symbols with no such structure, so their sinusoidal oscillations add noise rather than signal.
GMTU's Gaussian damping envelope zeroes out large activations, potentially suppressing high-magnitude semantically salient tokens.Turbulent's batch-level normalisation couples activations across the batch dimension, conflicting with NanoChat's causal masking. This occurs additional overhead making it costly. GMTU similar to turbulent also faces significant overhead and is quite costly, making it inefficient.



\subsection{Multi-Token Prediction}
MTP with $n=4$ heads shows improved performance as seen in Figure~\ref{fig:act_chatcore}.
On the aggregate ChatCORE metric, MTP (orange) performs comparably to the $\mathrm{ReLU}^2$ baseline.
On the ChatCORE-Categorical sub-score (ARC-Easy, ARC-Challenge, MMLU), MTP achieves a modest
improvement over the baseline, suggesting that the multi-horizon training objective encourages
richer representations for knowledge-retrieval tasks.
However, MTP incurs a significant training efficiency penalty, recording the \textbf{lowest
tokens-per-second} of all variants tested, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:act_tps}.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{images/activations_multi_tps.png}
  \caption{Training throughput (tokens per second) across activation variants and MTP.}
  \label{fig:act_tps}
\end{figure}

The improved ChatCORE-Categorical performance on ARC and MMLU is consistent with the hypothesis of \textcite{gloeckle2024better} that multi-horizon prediction forces the model to build more structured, fact-oriented representations. Correctly predicting four tokens ahead requires the model to capture syntactic and semantic structure beyond the immediate next word, and this is reflected in the marginal improvement on knowledge retrieval benchmarks.

However, the four output heads increase GPU memory pressure, reducing the effective memory available for batch processing and forcing a smaller batch size. The original MTP paper uses models trained on hundreds of billions of tokens, thus, the extra prediction heads have enough data to improve the model. At only 5,000 iterations, there is not enough training to justify the cost as the gains stay small

\subsection{Attention Residuals}
Replacing fixed additive residuals with learned AttnRes \parencite{chen2026attnres} yields
\textbf{worse performance on both validation BPB and ChatCORE} compared to the baseline,
as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:attn_res}.
Neither metric shows improvement at any point during training.

AttnRes is designed to prevent signal weakening in very deep models (40–80 layers), where early-layer information degrades as it passes through many residual connections. At only 12 layers, this problem barely exists, so AttnRes addresses something NanoChat does not suffer from. Additionally, its extra learnable parameters need significantly more training to converge. At 5,000 steps they remain underfit, leaving AttnRes in a worse state than the well-established standard residual baseline.

Figure~\ref{fig:engram} illustrates the two most diagnostic training signals.
During pretraining, all four configurations converge to the same validation BPB ($\approx 0.35$),
with essentially overlapping loss curves, confirming that Engram adds no measurable benefit to
pretraining quality at this scale.
After SFT, the Engram model shows a \textbf{severe degradation on the SpellingBee benchmark}
(from $0.95$ to $0.55$ without FP8), while baseline configurations remain near perfect.
Engram also performs worse on HumanEval.
On knowledge-intensive categorical tasks (ARC, MMLU), Engram variants perform similarly to or
marginally above the non-FP8 baseline, suggesting that the \(n\)-gram memory store helps improve on tasks that require recollection of known facts but lead to performance degradation in sequential generative tasks.

\subsection{FP8 Mixed-Precision Training}
FP8 was not a primary experiment but emerged as the strongest secondary finding.
Across both Engram and baseline runs, FP8 consistently outperforms full-precision training on
every metric without any architectural change.
The FP8 baseline is the strongest overall configuration, achieving ChatCORE $\approx 0.26$,
SpellingBee $\approx 1.0$, and HumanEval $\approx 0.08$.

This result is unexpected because FP8 reduces numerical precision and one would expect rounding
errors to hurt rather than help model quality. Since batch size was fixed across all configurations,
the gain cannot be attributed to larger effective batches.
The most plausible explanation is \textbf{quantisation noise as implicit regularisation}: the
rounding errors introduced by 8-bit arithmetic act as a stochastic perturbation of the gradient,
similar in spirit to dropout or weight noise.
Given the limited training iterations of 5{,}000 steps, this noise discourages the model from overfitting
to training corpus, effectively acting as a free regulariser.

This leads us to an interesting finding that at small
scale and limited data, mild stochasticity in training is beneficial, and precision reduction may
be a simple, cost-free way to achieve it.
